***REMOVED***% blocktrans trimmed %***REMOVED*** Sunday, 1 September 2019:
15:00
The wedding day will begin with the traditional Russian Orthodox crowning ceremony in the Church Of the Holy Righteous Simeon and Anna the Prophetess. The church was built in 1734 by Empress Anna Ioannovna in the heart of Saint Petersburg.
The ceremony will start with the bethrothal service, during which the priest blesses the rings. After which the couple exchanges them, placing them on their right hand.
This is immediately followed by the wedding service. The bride and groom will carry candles as the priest joins their hands whilst praying for their marriage. The candle- and hand-holding will continue until the end of the ceremony as a symbol of union.
Now comes the moment for the centerpiece of the Orthodox wedding ceremony: the Crowning. The crowns, held above the heads of the bride and the groom by the witnesses, symbolize the glory and honor that is being bestowed on them by God during the holy sacrament of mariage.
After the crowns have been placed firmly on the couple's heads, the bride and groom take three sips of wine from a shared cup. The wine is reminiscent of the miracle of the wedding of Cana of Galilee, where water was famously turned into wine.
Finally, the bride and groom will circle the central altar three times, echoing a pre-Christian ritual dance, accompagnied by a choir. In this act, the couple takes their first steps as a married couple and the ceremony is concluded with the ancient blessing "Na zisete" ("May you live!"***REMOVED*** ***REMOVED***% endblocktrans %***REMOVED***

After seeing the lush, rich, Imperial Saint Petersburg, you will get the opportunity to see the other side of the city: underground, vintage, and artistic. The reception will take place in the Craft Loft “Vesseniy”, not 15 minutes away by car. You'll be greeted with bubbles, canapés, and music. Shortly after which, the banquet will be served.
Russian wedding traditions are numerous and rich, a few you may want to know and participate in are as follows.
You may overhear guests talking about the bitterness of the food. This is no culinary critique, but one of the traditional wedding games. Guests slowly start to chant "Gorka, gorka, gorka..." (meaning "bitter"***REMOVED*** until the newlyweds kiss after which the guests start counting upwards. The goal being to kiss for as long as possible and then exclaim: "No, it's sweet!".
During the long banquet, all guests are welcome to perform a speech, song, dance, roast, or any other variety of number, in groups or alone. With multiple cultures and languages present, this is a relaxed and informal opportunity to exchange and bring the party together.
